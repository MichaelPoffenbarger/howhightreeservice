  • Significant trunk damage: Large cracks, seams, or dead branch stubs on the main trunk indicate internal decay.
  • Hollow trunk: If 30% or more of the trunk is hollow or rotted, removal is typically necessary.
  • Major deadwood: If more than 50% of the tree is dead or dying, it's often more practical to remove it.
  • Root damage: Severed or damaged roots can compromise the tree's stability.
  • Leaning tree: A sudden lean of more than 15 degrees indicates root breakage and imminent falling risk.
  • Disease or pest infestation: Advanced cases that can't be treated or might spread to other trees.

  1. Site assessment: Evaluating the tree, surrounding structures, and potential hazards.
  2. Creating a removal plan: Determining the safest direction for felling or sectional dismantling.
  3. Setting up safety perimeters: Securing the work area to protect people and property.
  4. Removing branches: Systematically cutting branches from bottom to top.
  5. Trunk removal: Either felling the trunk in one piece or cutting it into sections.
  6. Stump management: Either grinding the stump or treating it to prevent regrowth.
  7. Cleanup: Removing all debris and wood from the property or processing it for reuse.
